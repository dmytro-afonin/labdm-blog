## How it started

I am a dev. A web dev. One of those rare cases with a real software engineering degree — yep. Never used it though.

I started my career path in my last year at university. It was 2015. There were three of us in a group who could really write code. Both of my friends were already employed; I felt left behind… because I sucked at English and could not pass any interview. I barely knew English at all. So one day one of my friends gave me a contact — she said a friend of hers was looking for devs, and they did not care about English. I was excited!!

So I went. There were two dudes (bikers) in front of some apartments they were renting. They were freelancers who did stuff for the local market. The first question they asked was whether I knew PHP and JS — I said, "No, I know PHP and Java." They replied they did not need Java, but I should learn JS, and gave me a task: "Build an image slider — if you can, you can come back." (Yep, the golden era of programming: no libs, no package managers, no frameworks yet — only jQuery, which I also did not know existed yet.) So I came back the next day with a working slider, and they gave me a laptop and invited me into the apartments. The work was really hard — we shipped like crazy for very little money. Maybe I will write a more detailed post about this someday. For today, that is how my journey started.

## Fast‑forward to 2021

I grew into a full‑stack dev. I finished many projects, switched a couple of companies, and finally moved to the capital of my country — open to new opportunities. The opportunities did not make me wait long. Just a few months in, I received an offer from a big company — 2.5× better than what I had at the moment. The catch: it was a UI dev position. I quickly talked with our CTO; he offered me a counter‑offer and said the big company would kill my potential. I did not listen to him and explained that I had always wanted to get into big tech, so I left.

A few years later I understood what our former CTO had meant. I saw what a big company is. I became just a worker, with very little room to grow my technical skills. The good part, though — it gave me a chance to grow my soft skills! I finally became better at English, learned how to explain and argue for decisions (something I almost never did in small product companies), and learned how to work with junior devs — I had almost never seen junior devs before :) But I still missed the chance to do something cool, the kind of things I used to do… and I did not have time to do much else after work. So I gradually became more and more bored — and burned out.
